# This Meeting Has Points — Domain Glossary

## Card

A game card consisting of a `phrase`, `points`, optional `stringMatch`, and optional `alternates`.

- **phrase** — the exact string a player must say verbatim in the meeting. Not flavor text.
- **stringMatch** — the string a guesser types to identify a card (defaults to phrase if omitted).
- **alternates** — acceptable alternate spellings, hyphenation variants, or common shorthand for the stringMatch.
- **points** — the card's tier value (5, 10, 15, or 30).

## Tiers

Difficulty is determined by two axes: **phrase length** (primary at the Easy end) and **how naturally the phrase fits in a corporate meeting** (primary from Medium up).

| Tier | Points | Length | Context fit | Design rule |
|------|--------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Easy | 5 | 1–3 words | Completely natural | Short, not boring. Corporate, general idiom, or edgy — anything unremarkable when said. Avoid flat single-word jargon. |
| Medium | 10 | Any | Plausible with finesse | "A little weird." Has voice. Often the less-obvious version of something familiar. |
| Hard | 15 | Any | Requires creative setup | Requires audacity or preamble to land. Often full sentences. You have to commit to say it. |
| Expert | 30 | Any | Always a non-sequitur | No setup makes it not weird. Audacity alone gets you through. |

## The boring problem

The primary failure mode for Easy cards is being **boring** — a flat corporate vocabulary word with no personality. Short is necessary but not sufficient. "Aggressive timeline" is Easy. "Synergy" alone is not.

## The sassy meme problem

The global tone failure mode across all tiers: phrases that read as pre-packaged wit — the kind of thing printed on a Redbubble t-shirt or shared as a reaction GIF. These feel performed rather than said. Cards should feel like something a person genuinely said, not a meme they recited. "In my defense, I was left unsupervised" fails this filter. "I did not have time to prepare for this meeting" passes it.

## Expert card craft

Expert cards need specificity and a phrase wrapper — not just a flat noun or name drop. "Mick Fleetwood" beats "Fleetwood Mac" (more obscure). "She's a competitive knitter." beats "competitive knitting" (committed to the bit). "Seventeen ugly-assed pelicans" beats "seventeen pelicans" (the modifier is the joke). Even absurdist content benefits from craft.

## Phrase format

Phrases get shorter at Easy and longer as difficulty increases — but Expert can be short if the phrase is inherently a non-sequitur (e.g., "Sufjan Stevens"). Length is a proxy for Easy; non-sequitur-ness is the axis for Expert.

Phrases that read as sentences should be capitalized and punctuated as sentences. "I volunteer as tribute!" not "I volunteer as tribute". This applies across all tiers.
